1st look at Maori language statistics from New Zealand Census Data

(2014-2-4)

Maori language stats from New Zealand Census data (mostly 2001,2006, 2013)

Due to the Christchurch earthquake in 2011, the time series is irregular. I am interested in trends, not absolute numbers which contain both error and probably over reporting on the language question.

Figure 1 displays the number of Maori by descent, ethnicity and Maori language speakers (only those claiming Maori ethnicity), from 1991 and includes a rough projection to 2021, the most likely date of the next New Zealand census.

Question on language use has only been asked since 2001. Figure 1, shows there has been a reasonably consistent increase in the number of Maori claiming Maori descent and Maori ethnicity, but a reasonably steady decrease in the number of Maori language speakers.

Figure 2 displays Maori language speakers (only those of Maori ethnicity) by 4 age groups. This clearly shows that in terms of absolute numbers there have been reasonably consistent decreases in all age groups (from 2001 to 2013), with the exception of those of over the age of 64.

Figure 3 displays the number of Maori language speakers by Region, from 2001 to 2013. There was a consistent decrease across all regions since 2006. The Bay of Plenty, Gisborne, Waikato and Northland have always had large Maori populations. Auckland now has a large number of Maori residents as a result of internal migration.

Figure 4 displays the percentage of Maori language speakers by region. I.e., what percentage of the Maori population (in that region) claim to be speakers of Maori. There are larger percentages of Maori speakers where there are higher percentages of Maori, although the reasons for this are historic and largely reflect delayed language shift in rural areas where Maori communities are still intact.

Figure 5 displays percentage of speakers by the percentage of the population for each region as a scatter plot. In some ways this is kind of collapsing or combining figures 3 and 4. There is a positive correlation between these variables.

In some cases regional analyses can be misleading, as there is a lot of variation in regions such as Auckland and the Bay of Plenty. This needs to be explored by looking at next level, i.e., District Councils (yes I am planning to do this).

Where to from here ? First, I'll look at iwi (Maori tribes). I have requested additional data from Stats NZ, but will not get it until the end of March or later. Then I'll look at 'defacto' social class measures such as income and education. I will also add data on Maori and Maori speakers in Australia.

Figures were done in R (ggplot2) using RStudio on a MacBook Pro. Github repo is here. Figures were exported from R as PDFs and then re-sized and resolution increased in Illustrator.
